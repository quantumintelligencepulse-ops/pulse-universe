### 1. Developmental divisions – the five vesicles

These are the core “taxonomic roots” of the brain. Every major structure you’ve heard of grows out of these.

#### **Telencephalon** (forebrain – “end brain”)
- **What it becomes:**
  - Cerebral cortex (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital lobes)
  - Basal ganglia (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus)
  - Hippocampus
  - Amygdala
  - Olfactory bulb
- **What it does:**
  - Highest‑order cognition: planning, reasoning, language, abstract thought
  - Voluntary movement planning (motor cortex + basal ganglia loops)
  - Declarative memory (hippocampus)
  - Emotional salience and fear learning (amygdala)
  - Conscious perception and complex sensory integration
- **Why it matters:**
  - This is what people usually mean by “the brain” in everyday language.
  - Most psychiatric and cognitive disorders involve telencephalic circuits.

---

#### **Diencephalon** (between brain)
- **What it becomes:**
  - Thalamus
  - Hypothalamus
  - Epithalamus (includes pineal gland)
  - Subthalamus
- **What it does:**
  - **Thalamus:** major relay hub for almost all sensory information going to cortex (except smell); also involved in attention and consciousness.
  - **Hypothalamus:** homeostasis (temperature, hunger, thirst), endocrine control via pituitary, circadian rhythms, stress response.
  - **Epithalamus/pineal:** melatonin secretion, sleep–wake cycles.
  - **Subthalamus:** part of motor control loops with basal ganglia.
- **Why it matters:**
  - The diencephalon is the **control center** for internal state and sensory routing.
  - Damage here can disrupt consciousness, sleep, appetite, temperature, hormones.

---

#### **Mesencephalon** (midbrain)
- **What it becomes:**
  - Tectum (superior and inferior colliculi)
  - Tegmentum (periaqueductal gray, red nucleus, substantia nigra, etc.)
- **What it does:**
  - **Superior colliculus:** orienting movements of eyes/head toward visual stimuli.
  - **Inferior colliculus:** auditory processing and sound localization.
  - **Substantia nigra:** dopamine production for movement and reward (degenerates in Parkinson’s).
  - **Periaqueductal gray:** pain modulation, defensive behaviors.
- **Why it matters:**
  - It’s a **sensorimotor integration hub**—fast orienting, reflexive responses, dopaminergic control of movement and motivation.

---

#### **Metencephalon** (after brain – part of hindbrain)
- **What it becomes:**
  - Pons
  - Cerebellum
- **What it does:**
  - **Pons:** bridge between cortex, cerebellum, and spinal cord; involved in sleep, arousal, facial expressions, eye movements.
  - **Cerebellum:** fine‑tunes movement, balance, coordination, motor learning; also contributes to timing and some cognitive functions.
- **Why it matters:**
  - Cerebellum is critical for **precision and learning of motor skills**.
  - Pons is a major relay and part of the sleep/arousal system.

---

#### **Myelencephalon** (spinal‑like brain – hindbrain)
- **What it becomes:**
  - Medulla oblongata
- **What it does:**
  - Controls **vital autonomic functions**:
    - Heart rate
    - Blood pressure
    - Breathing
    - Swallowing, vomiting reflexes
- **Why it matters:**
  - This is the “you die if this fails” region.
  - Many basic life‑support reflexes live here.

---

### 2. Major structural divisions – forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain

This is the “big map” that sits on top of the developmental story.

#### **Forebrain**
- **Includes:**
  - Telencephalon (cortex, basal ganglia, limbic structures)
  - Diencephalon (thalamus, hypothalamus, etc.)
- **Functions:**
  - Conscious thought, voluntary movement, memory, emotion, decision‑making, sensory perception, internal state regulation.

#### **Midbrain**
- **Includes:**
  - Mesencephalon structures (tectum, tegmentum, substantia nigra, etc.)
- **Functions:**
  - Reflexive orienting, basic motor control, dopamine systems, pain modulation.

#### **Hindbrain**
- **Includes:**
  - Metencephalon (pons, cerebellum)
  - Myelencephalon (medulla)
- **Functions:**
  - Coordination, balance, motor learning, sleep, arousal, autonomic life‑support.

### 3. Functional systems – how the brain is actually used

This is how cognitive neuroscience carves things up.

#### **Sensory systems**
- **Visual:** retina → thalamus (LGN) → occipital cortex.
- **Auditory:** cochlea → brainstem → thalamus (MGN) → temporal cortex.
- **Somatosensory:** skin/joints → spinal cord → thalamus → parietal cortex.
- **Olfactory:** olfactory bulb → piriform cortex (bypasses thalamus initially).
- **Gustatory:** taste buds → brainstem → thalamus → insula.
- **Role:** build internal models of the external world.

#### **Motor systems**
- **Primary motor cortex:** sends commands to spinal cord.
- **Premotor/supplementary motor areas:** plan and sequence movements.
- **Basal ganglia:** action selection, initiation, habit formation.
- **Cerebellum:** error correction, timing, coordination.
- **Role:** transform goals and plans into precise muscle activity.

#### **Limbic system**
- **Amygdala:** emotional salience, especially fear and threat.
- **Hippocampus:** episodic memory, spatial navigation.
- **Cingulate cortex:** conflict monitoring, emotion–cognition interface.
- **Nucleus accumbens / ventral striatum:** reward, motivation.
- **Role:** value, emotion, memory, motivation.

#### **Autonomic / homeostatic systems**
- **Hypothalamus:** hunger, thirst, temperature, hormones, circadian rhythms.
- **Brainstem nuclei:** heart rate, breathing, blood pressure.
- **Role:** keep the body alive and in balance.

#### **Association networks**
- **Default Mode Network:** self‑referential thought, mind‑wandering, autobiographical memory.
- **Salience Network:** detects important internal/external events, switches attention.
- **Executive Control Network:** working memory, planning, cognitive control.
- **Role:** high‑level integration, decision‑making, flexible behavior.

### 4. Cortical microstructure – the six layers

These are the **laminae** of the neocortex, especially important in sensory and association areas.

1. **Layer I – Molecular layer**
   - Mostly dendrites and axons, very few cell bodies.
   - Integrative layer; receives feedback and modulatory input.

2. **Layer II – External granular layer**
   - Small granular (stellate) neurons.
   - Receives input from other cortical areas; local processing.

3. **Layer III – External pyramidal layer**
   - Small to medium pyramidal neurons.
   - Major source of **corticocortical projections** (communication between cortical areas).

4. **Layer IV – Internal granular layer**
   - Densely packed stellate cells.
   - Primary **input layer** from thalamus (especially in sensory cortex).
   - Very prominent in primary sensory areas (e.g., V1).

5. **Layer V – Internal pyramidal layer**
   - Large pyramidal neurons (e.g., Betz cells in motor cortex).
   - Major **output layer** to spinal cord, brainstem, basal ganglia.
   - Critical for voluntary movement.

6. **Layer VI – Multiform (fusiform) layer**
   - Heterogeneous cell types.
   - Sends feedback projections to thalamus.
   - Helps regulate thalamocortical loops.

Different cortical areas vary in how thick or prominent each layer is (e.g., motor cortex has huge layer V; sensory cortex has huge layer IV).
