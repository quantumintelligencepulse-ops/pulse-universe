## 1. Developmental divisions – how the brain is built

These five vesicles are the **true root categories**. Everything else grows out of them.

### Telencephalon

**What it becomes:**

- **Cerebral cortex**  
  - Frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital lobes  
- **Basal ganglia**  
  - Caudate, putamen, globus pallidus  
- **Limbic structures**  
  - Hippocampus, amygdala, parts of cingulate cortex  
- **Olfactory bulb**

**What it actually does:**

- **Cortex:**
  - **Frontal lobe:** planning, decision‑making, working memory, inhibition, personality, voluntary motor control.
  - **Parietal lobe:** spatial awareness, body schema, integrating sensory info, attention.
  - **Temporal lobe:** auditory processing, language comprehension (Wernicke’s area), object recognition, memory encoding.
  - **Occipital lobe:** visual processing (edges → shapes → objects → scenes).

- **Basal ganglia:**
  - Action selection: deciding which motor program (or cognitive strategy) to execute.
  - Reinforcement learning: dopamine signals from midbrain teach which actions are rewarded.
  - Habit formation: repeated actions become automatic loops.

- **Hippocampus:**
  - Forms new episodic memories (events, places, contexts).
  - Spatial navigation (place cells, grid cells).
  - Consolidation: helps transfer memories to long‑term cortical storage.

- **Amygdala:**
  - Tags stimuli with emotional salience (especially fear, threat, reward).
  - Rapid threat detection and conditioned fear learning.
  - Modulates memory strength based on emotional intensity.

**How it develops and evolves:**

- Telencephalon expands massively in humans, especially the **neocortex**.
- Cortical folding (gyri/sulci) increases surface area without needing a bigger skull.
- Prefrontal cortex (part of frontal lobe) is one of the last regions to fully mature (into mid‑20s), which tracks with impulse control and long‑term planning.

---

### Diencephalon

**What it becomes:**

- **Thalamus**
- **Hypothalamus**
- **Epithalamus** (pineal gland)
- **Subthalamus**

**What it actually does:**

- **Thalamus:**
  - Main relay station for sensory info going to cortex (vision, hearing, touch, taste—not smell).
  - Each sensory modality has its own thalamic nucleus (e.g., LGN for vision, MGN for audition).
  - Also involved in attention and consciousness—thalamocortical loops are critical for wakefulness.

- **Hypothalamus:**
  - Master regulator of **homeostasis**:
    - Body temperature
    - Hunger and satiety
    - Thirst
    - Circadian rhythms
  - Controls the **pituitary gland**, which controls hormones (stress, growth, reproduction, thyroid, etc.).
  - Integrates signals from body (hormones, blood osmolarity, temperature) and brain (emotion, stress).

- **Epithalamus / Pineal gland:**
  - Secretes melatonin.
  - Regulates sleep–wake cycles and seasonal rhythms.

- **Subthalamus:**
  - Part of motor control circuits with basal ganglia.
  - Involved in suppressing unwanted movements.

**Developmental note:**

- Diencephalon sits between brainstem and cortex—**everything passes through it**.
- It’s a central hub for both sensory routing and internal state regulation.

---

### Mesencephalon (Midbrain)

**What it becomes:**

- **Tectum**
  - Superior colliculus
  - Inferior colliculus
- **Tegmentum**
  - Periaqueductal gray (PAG)
  - Red nucleus
  - Substantia nigra
  - Ventral tegmental area (VTA)

**What it actually does:**

- **Superior colliculus:**
  - Rapid orienting of eyes and head toward visual stimuli.
  - Integrates visual, auditory, and somatosensory info for reflexive movements.

- **Inferior colliculus:**
  - Processes auditory information.
  - Important for sound localization and reflexive responses to sound.

- **Substantia nigra:**
  - Dopamine production for motor circuits (nigrostriatal pathway).
  - Degeneration here → Parkinson’s disease (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia).

- **Ventral tegmental area (VTA):**
  - Dopamine neurons projecting to limbic system and cortex (mesolimbic and mesocortical pathways).
  - Central to reward, motivation, addiction.

- **Periaqueductal gray:**
  - Pain modulation (endogenous opioids).
  - Defensive behaviors (freezing, fight/flight patterns).

**Developmental note:**

- Mesencephalon is evolutionarily older and heavily conserved.
- It’s a **sensorimotor and motivational hub**—fast, reflexive, survival‑oriented.

---

### Metencephalon

**What it becomes:**

- **Pons**
- **Cerebellum**

**What it actually does:**

- **Pons:**
  - Relay between cortex and cerebellum.
  - Contains nuclei involved in sleep, arousal, facial expressions, eye movements.
  - Part of the reticular formation (arousal and consciousness).

- **Cerebellum:**
  - Fine‑tunes movement: balance, coordination, precision.
  - Compares intended movement (from cortex) with actual movement (from sensory feedback).
  - Motor learning: adapts movements based on error signals.
  - Also contributes to timing, rhythm, and some cognitive functions (language, attention).

**Developmental note:**

- Cerebellum has its own layered cortex and deep nuclei.
- It contains an enormous number of neurons (more than the rest of the brain combined), mostly granule cells.

---

### Myelencephalon

**What it becomes:**

- **Medulla oblongata**

**What it actually does:**

- Controls **vital autonomic functions**:
  - Heart rate
  - Blood pressure
  - Breathing rhythm
  - Swallowing, coughing, vomiting reflexes
- Contains nuclei for cranial nerves that control face, tongue, and visceral organs.

**Developmental note:**

- This is the most “life‑critical” region—damage here is often fatal.
- It’s continuous with the spinal cord.

---

## 2. Major structural divisions – forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain

This is the big anatomical map built on top of those developmental roots.

### Forebrain

**Includes:**

- Telencephalon (cortex, basal ganglia, limbic structures)
- Diencephalon (thalamus, hypothalamus, etc.)

**Functions:**

- Conscious thought, voluntary movement, perception, memory, emotion, decision‑making.
- Integration of sensory input with internal state and goals.
- Planning, reasoning, language, social cognition.

---

### Midbrain

**Includes:**

- Mesencephalon structures (tectum, tegmentum, substantia nigra, VTA, PAG)

**Functions:**

- Rapid orienting to stimuli.
- Basic motor control and posture.
- Dopamine systems for reward and movement.
- Pain modulation and defensive behaviors.

---

### Hindbrain

**Includes:**

- Metencephalon (pons, cerebellum)
- Myelencephalon (medulla)

**Functions:**

- Coordination, balance, motor learning.
- Sleep and arousal.
- Autonomic life‑support (breathing, heart rate, blood pressure).

---

## 3. Functional systems – how the brain is used in behavior

This is how cognitive neuroscience slices the brain.

### Sensory systems

**Visual system:**

- Retina → optic nerve → lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN, thalamus) → primary visual cortex (V1) → higher visual areas (V2, V4, MT, etc.).
- Processes edges, orientation, motion, color, depth, objects, faces, scenes.

**Auditory system:**

- Cochlea → brainstem nuclei → inferior colliculus → medial geniculate nucleus (MGN, thalamus) → primary auditory cortex.
- Processes frequency, timing, location, complex sounds (speech, music).

**Somatosensory system:**

- Receptors in skin, muscles, joints → spinal cord → brainstem → ventral posterior nucleus (thalamus) → primary somatosensory cortex (S1).
- Encodes touch, pressure, vibration, temperature, pain, body position.

**Olfactory system:**

- Olfactory receptors → olfactory bulb → piriform cortex, amygdala, entorhinal cortex.
- Unique: bypasses thalamus initially.
- Strong links to memory and emotion.

**Gustatory system:**

- Taste buds → brainstem → thalamus → insular cortex.
- Encodes taste qualities (sweet, salty, sour, bitter, umami).

---

### Motor systems

**Primary motor cortex (M1):**

- Directly sends commands to spinal motor neurons.
- Somatotopic map (motor homunculus).

**Premotor and supplementary motor areas:**

- Plan and sequence movements.
- Coordinate bilateral actions.
- Translate goals into motor programs.

**Basal ganglia:**

- Loops with cortex and thalamus.
- Selects which actions to initiate or suppress.
- Uses dopamine to encode reward prediction errors.

**Cerebellum:**

- Compares intended vs. actual movement.
- Adjusts motor commands to reduce error.
- Critical for learning new motor skills (e.g., riding a bike).

---

### Limbic system

**Amygdala:**

- Rapid evaluation of emotional significance.
- Fear conditioning, threat detection.
- Modulates memory consolidation based on emotional intensity.

**Hippocampus:**

- Encodes new episodic memories.
- Spatial maps and navigation.
- Pattern separation (distinguishing similar experiences).

**Cingulate cortex:**

- Monitors conflict, pain, and emotional states.
- Links emotion to decision‑making.

**Nucleus accumbens / ventral striatum:**

- Reward, motivation, reinforcement learning.
- Central in addiction pathways.

---

### Autonomic / homeostatic systems

**Hypothalamus:**

- Integrates signals from body and brain.
- Controls endocrine system via pituitary.
- Regulates hunger, thirst, temperature, sexual behavior, circadian rhythms.

**Brainstem autonomic nuclei:**

- Medulla and pons control heart rate, blood pressure, respiration.
- Reflexes like coughing, vomiting, swallowing.

---

### Association networks

**Default Mode Network (DMN):**

- Medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, angular gyrus, etc.
- Active during rest, mind‑wandering, self‑referential thought, autobiographical memory.

**Salience Network:**

- Anterior insula, dorsal anterior cingulate.
- Detects important internal/external events.
- Switches between DMN and executive networks.

**Executive Control Network:**

- Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, posterior parietal cortex.
- Working memory, planning, cognitive control, task management.

These networks are **distributed**, not localized to one structure.

## 4. Cortical microstructure – the six layers in depth

The neocortex is a 6‑layer sheet, ~2–4 mm thick, with different cell types and connectivity.

### Layer I – Molecular layer

- Mostly dendrites and axons from deeper layers and other regions.
- Very few neuron cell bodies.
- Integrates feedback and modulatory input from higher‑order areas and thalamus.

### Layer II – External granular layer

- Small granular (stellate) neurons.
- Receives input from other cortical areas.
- Important for local processing and horizontal connections.

### Layer III – External pyramidal layer

- Small to medium pyramidal neurons.
- Major source of **corticocortical projections** (to other cortical regions, both same and opposite hemisphere).
- Critical for communication between different cortical areas.

### Layer IV – Internal granular layer

- Densely packed stellate cells.
- Primary **input layer** from thalamus.
- Very prominent in primary sensory cortices (e.g., V1, S1).
- Thalamocortical axons terminate heavily here.

### Layer V – Internal pyramidal layer

- Large pyramidal neurons (e.g., Betz cells in motor cortex).
- Major **output layer** to:
  - Spinal cord
  - Brainstem
  - Basal ganglia
- Critical for voluntary motor control and subcortical influence.

### Layer VI – Multiform (fusiform) layer

- Heterogeneous cell types.
- Sends feedback projections to thalamus.
- Helps regulate thalamocortical loops and gating of sensory input.

**Regional variation:**

- **Motor cortex:** thick layer V (big output), thinner layer IV.
- **Sensory cortex:** thick layer IV (big input), thinner layer V.
- **Association cortex:** more balanced layers, heavy II/III for corticocortical communication.
